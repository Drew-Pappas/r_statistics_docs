The Data

The dataset for this assignment was gathered in 1970 in the Boston metropolitan area. Each row corresponds to one US Census tract. These are geographic areas with a few thousand people used by the U.S. government in it’s decennial census of the population. (The help menu entry refers to “towns” in a few places. These should read “census tracts”.) The dataset is the Boston dataset in the MASS package.

Note that the set of entries here is not a sample of a larger population of Boston census tracts. It represents all census tracts in Boston (Though each entry itself may be a sample of the larger population in that tract.) We also can not say that Boston represents the entire US, since regions differ. For our purposes, let us assume that these census tracts are a sample of all census tracts in urban areas in the northeastern United States.

Q3 Response

In order to compute a t-test with the traditional method, we must check for independence in the collection of observations and approximate normality in the sample. Although not specified, the ptratio appears to pass the independence check because the census tracts are based geographically. Assuming there is no overlap in geography where places are being sampled twice, we can say that these observations are independent.

As for approximate normality of the distribution, the sample is CLOSE to normal depending on your binwidth selected for the histogram. To visualize this difference I used ggplot to create two histograms, one with a binwidth of 1.5 and the other with a binwidth of 1. With a binwidth of 1.5 we see a distribution that looks relatively normal. However, with a 1 binwidth the data begins to stray from normality. It’s unclear whether we should use a t-test here, but because this isn’t an egregiously non-normal distribution and there is a larger sample size, the test can be performed.

To compute the 95% confidence interval using traditional methods I used the formula xbar +- t * s/sqrt(n). To find those values, I used the summarize function to calculate mean, standard deviation, and n. From there, I calculated the standard error using the formula sigma/sqrt(n) and found the upper and lower bounds by using the first formula. The values for the lower and upper bounds forming the confidence interval are 18.2669 and 18.64417 respectively. Thus, we are 95% confident that the true population mean of pupil to teacher ratios in the urban northeastern United States is between 18.2669 and 18.64417.

Question 8 (10 Points)

Remember how the distribution of per capita crime looked? This means that the data is right-skewed. In cases like this we might try log-transforming the data. If we find a stronger statistical relationship with the log-transformed data, then there might be an exponential process that generated the original data. (Because logarithms undo exponents.) For example if you suspect that the relationship between variables x and y is \(y=a*b^x\), then the log-transformed equation is \(log(y)=log(a)+x*log(b)\) or, rewriting the constants, \(log(y)=A + Bx\). So x and log(y) will be linearly related, and we can do a linear regression. For more more details on logarithms, you can check out: https://www.statisticshowto.datasciencecentral.com/probability-and-statistics/statistics-definitions/logarithms/
